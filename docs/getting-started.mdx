This page explains how gitcounsel is put together, so the rest of this documentation
makes sense. You do not need any technical knowledge to read it.

## The main idea: everything is written down

Think of gitcounsel as a document system with a perfect memory. Every time something
changes — a contract is edited, a cell in a table is filled in, a saved set of steps
is updated — gitcounsel writes down a record of that change. Each record tells you:

- **Who** made the change. This is either a named person on your team, or an AI
  assistant acting on someone's behalf.
- **What** changed. The record keeps the exact wording before and after, so you can
  compare them.
- **When** it happened. The date and time are saved.
- **Why** it happened, if a note was added to explain it.

Because of this, you never lose your history. You can always trace any word, number,
or date back to the moment it was set and see who set it. If you have used "track
changes" in a word processor, this is the same idea, applied to everything in the
product instead of just one document.

## How your work is organized

gitcounsel follows the way law firms already work. There are three layers:

1. **Client.** The company or person you act for, such as a business you represent.
2. **Matter.** A single case or project for that client, for example "Acme
   acquisition" or "Office lease renewal." Each member of your team is added to the
   specific matters they work on.
3. **Your work.** Documents, contract reviews, tables, and saved steps all live inside
   a matter.

So the path is always **Client, then Matter, then your work**. Anyone added to a
matter can see and work on everything inside it, and every action they take is saved
under their name.

## Two ways to work

- **In the app.** Sign in, open a matter, and use the screens to review documents,
  build tables, run your saved steps, and chat about your files.
- **Through your own AI assistant.** Connect a tool like Claude or ChatGPT to
  gitcounsel. You ask it in plain language, for example "review these NDAs," and it
  does the work in gitcounsel for you. Everything it does is written down in the same
  way as before. The only difference is that the record shows the AI assistant's name
  instead of a person's name. See [AI &amp; agents](/ai-agents) to learn how to set
  this up.

## Signing in

gitcounsel uses an email address and a password. Your administrator creates your
account for you. Once you sign in, you will land on the list of matters you are part
of. If your firm runs gitcounsel on its own servers, see
[Self-hosting setup](/admin/self-hosting).
