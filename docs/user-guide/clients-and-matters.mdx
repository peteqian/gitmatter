---
title: Clients & matters
description: How your work is organized — by the client you act for and the case you act on.
---

In gitcounsel, every piece of work sits under two things: a **client** and a
**matter**. This is the same way a firm already files its work, so there is almost
nothing new to learn. If you know who the work is for and what the work is about,
you already know how to find it.

## Clients

A client is the company or person you act for — for example, a business you advise
or an individual you represent.

The **Clients** screen is your starting point. From it you can:

- See a list of every client your firm works with.
- Add a new client.
- Click into a client to see all the matters opened for them.

Think of each client as a folder that holds all the cases you handle for that one
company or person.

## Matters

A matter is a single case or project for a client — for example, "Acme acquisition"
or "Q3 vendor contracts". This is where your real, day-to-day work happens. A matter
holds the documents, the reviews, and your conversations with the assistant.

When you open a matter, you can:

- See the documents filed under it, and upload more.
- Start a tabular review (reading the same question across many documents) or run a
  workflow. See [Tabular review](/user-guide/tabular-review).
- Ask the assistant questions about the documents in the matter.
- Change the matter's details — its name, its client, or its jurisdiction — using the
  pencil button.
- Close the matter once the work is finished.

### Jurisdiction

Each matter has a **jurisdiction**. This simply means the place whose laws apply to
the matter — for example, a particular US state.

Some tools, such as legal case-law research, only switch on for matters in a place
that gitcounsel supports. So it is worth setting the jurisdiction correctly: doing so
makes sure the right tools show up for that matter.

## Your team

People are added to a matter as members, and each person is given a role. Anyone
placed on the matter can work in it.

Here is the important part: **every action a person takes is recorded against their
name.** Who uploaded a document, who accepted an edit, who ran a review — all of it
is kept in the matter's history. This is what lets someone look back later and see
exactly who did what, and when.
